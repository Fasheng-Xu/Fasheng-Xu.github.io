01 / About

Fasheng Xu is an Associate Professor of Operations and Information Management at School of Business, University of Connecticut (UConn). Fasheng’s general research interests lie in the interface of operations, finance, and economics, with a particular focus on the economic and social implications of emerging technologies. His current research examines how Generative AI reshapes organizational structure and market dynamics, the rise of the agentic economy, the governance and optimization of agentic systems, and the economics and financing of AI supply chains.

His work has been published in top-tier academic journals such as Management Science and M&SOM, and he has received multiple best paper nominations along with prestigious research awards, including the 2025 Chelliah Sriskandarajah Early Career Research Accomplishments Award. At UConn, Fasheng designed and launched a new MBA course on Supply Chain Finance. He also recently co-authored a book, The Owner: A Business Novel about Work in the GenAI Era, which translates these research themes into narrative form for broader audiences.

Invited Workshop

PhD Workshop on GenAI and the Agentic Future

Two-day PhD Workshop · School of Management, Zhejiang University · Summer 2026

This workshop provides a broad overview of how generative AI is reshaping research, organizations, markets, and infrastructure. We begin by examining the state of AI in 2026, situating GenAI within the larger technological landscape, identifying the organizational barriers that limit adoption, and engaging the “bitter lesson” perspective on intelligence. We then turn to agentic research workflows, exploring how AI agents can support the research process, how researchers can build reusable agent skills, and what autonomous research may imply for the future of science itself. The workshop next considers the emerging agentic economy, where AI agents reshape organizational design, mediate commerce, and give rise to new forms of economic activity. Finally, we examine the AI supply chain, focusing on the infrastructure, financing, and systemic risks that make large-scale AI possible, with particular attention to data centers and the GPU economy. Together, these sections offer a big-picture framework for understanding GenAI not only as a tool, but as a transformative force across knowledge work, markets, organizations, and society.
